This page collects short reflections on how I approach game design as a solo developer. Rather than focusing on visual polish, I prioritize clarity of mechanics, player feedback, and iterative improvement through playtesting.

Context-Based Combat

Context: Traditional action games use multiple buttons for different attacks, increasing complexity and cognitive load for players.

Decision: I implemented a single attack input that varies based on player state (airborne, standing, moving), creating different attacks from one button.

Result: Players could focus on positioning and timing rather than memorizing button combinations, while still experiencing combat depth through emergent move combinations.

Insight: Simplicity in input controls can create depth through contextual variation. Less is often more when it comes to control schemes.

Physics Tuning by Feel

Context: Jump mechanics needed to feel satisfying and responsive, not just function correctly from a physics standpoint.

Decision: Rather than using mathematically "correct" physics values, I spent extensive time playtesting and tweaking gravity, jump height, and air control based purely on how it felt.

Result: Movement feels tight and enjoyable. Players have precise control, and jumps feel predictable yet dynamic. Hundreds of small adjustments transformed functional movement into fun movement.

Insight: The difference between functional and enjoyable often lies in fine-tuning through iteration. Feel trumps mathematical accuracy in game design.

Camera System Evolution

Context: My initial simple follow camera felt jarring during jumps and rapid player movement, creating a disorienting experience.

Decision: I refined the camera to anticipate player direction and smooth out rapid movements, looking slightly ahead in the direction of jumps and movement.

Result: The viewing experience became more cinematic and comfortable. Players could see ahead to where they're going, reducing frustration and improving platforming precision.
