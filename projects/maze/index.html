This project studies how procedural structure influences player navigation, confusion, and discovery.

Project Components

Randomly Generated Maze

A procedural maze generation system that creates unique layouts every time using algorithms like recursive backtracking and depth-first search. Each maze is guaranteed to be solvable, with a clear path from start to finish, but the complexity and layout vary with each generation. This randomness creates an ever-changing environment where players must navigate unfamiliar spaces.

Player Experience Focus

When did players feel lost? Players became disoriented in long, featureless corridors with too many similar-looking junctions. Without visual landmarks or memorable spatial features, they lost track of where they'd been and struggled to build a mental map of the space.

When did exploration feel rewarding? Discovery felt most satisfying when players found shortcuts, dead-ends with collectibles, or when they recognized a previously visited area from a new angle. These moments of spatial understanding created a sense of mastery over the maze's complexity.

What made layouts readable vs frustrating? Readable mazes had distinct sections with varied corridor lengths and recognizable junction patterns. Frustrating mazes felt homogeneousâ€”every turn looked identical, making navigation feel random rather than skill-based. The key difference was environmental variety that supported spatial memory.

Design Takeaway

This informed my level design approach in the platformer, especially in how I guide players subtly without explicit tutorials. I learned that spatial clarity comes from environmental variety and memorable landmarks, not from making paths obvious. In my platformer levels, I apply this by creating visually distinct areas, using environmental cues to hint at hidden paths, and ensuring players can build a mental map through visual consistency. The goal is to let players feel clever for finding the way forward, rather than feeling lost or hand-held.
